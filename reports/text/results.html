\subsubsection{Data Collection} [3]
Hospitalizations with a discharge date from January 2011 to May 2018 were extracted % [16]
from the Cleveland Clinic Foundation (CCF) electronic health record (EHR).\@ [26]
Clinical, demographic, and institutional features were extracted using natural language processing % [37]
and parsing of structured data available within the EHR.\@ [46]
A full list of variables used for each predictive goal is available in Tables S.X.\@ %TODO: make tables S.X [61]
Readmission was defined as any new CCF hospitalization starting 4 hours after any CCF discharge, % [76]
but before the predefined thresholds (3, 5, 7, and 30 days).\@ [87]
Length of stay was defined as the time between a given discharge and admission for each hospitalization.\@ [104]
Death within 48--72 hours of admission was defined as a recorded EHR, Social Security, % [119]
or Ohio Death Index death date, or a discharge disposition of ``expired,'' within the given time frame.\@ [136]
For prediction of readmission, patients whose discharge disposition was ``expired'' were removed.\@ [148]
Patients with an admission class of ``observation'' were retained, as it has been suggested that [163]
readmission reduction programs have resulted in an increased use of the observational setting.\supercite{obs2016nejm, obsincreasing2019} [178]
The 4 hour cutoff removed patients who were simply transferring from one CCF department or hospital % [194]
to another, and was selected based on histogram analysis of first-day readmissions.\@ [206]
For prediction of length of stay and death within 48--72 hours of admission, % [220]
only variables available within roughly 24 hours of admission were considered, % [231]
and patients with an admission class of ``observation'' were removed.\@ [241]

\subsubsection{Predictive Modeling} [243]
Gradient Boosting Decision Trees (GBDT), also known as [251]
Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM), were used to produce predictive models.\@ [261]
GBM is a nonparametric method that trains many decision trees in succession, using [274]
information from each set of decision trees to optimize the performance [285]
of the next iteration.\supercite{gbmtutorial} We used LightGBM v2.2.2, [294]
which allows for rapid training by utilizing a histogram-based method [304]
to discretize continuous variables, and achieves state-of-the-art performance in relation [314]
to other machine learning methods.\supercite{lightgbm}  It also allows for [324]
inclusion of many types of variables, and can explicitly [333]
account for missingness.\@ GBM models do not require feature scaling [343]
or imputation of missing values.\@ [348]
To reduce model overfitting, we employed a standard train/test/validation split [360]
and early stopping at 200 iterations.\@ [366]

\subsubsection{Model Explanations} [368]
Global and personalized model predictions were interpreted using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanation) values.\supercite{lundberg2017unified}\@ [382]
SHAP values, based on the Shapley value from coalitional game theory, [393]
are consistent and accurate calculations of the contributions of each feature to any machine learning model's prediction.\@ [410]
SHAP values may be used to explain a model globally, by examining the average contribution of a given feature [429]
to the model output, or locally, by examining the most important variables for a given prediction.\supercite{lundberg2018explainable} \@ [446]
They may also be used to examine interactions between variables.\@ [456]
SHAP values were generated using SHAP v0.28.5.\@ [463]
Visualizations were created using Matplotlib v3.0.3\supercite{matplotlib} and the \LaTeX\ typesetting language.\@ [475]
[475]

\subsubsection{Statistical Analysis} [477]
Baseline characteristics were reported as median [quartiles 1 and 3] and frequencies (\%), as appropriate, on a per-hospitalization (rather than per-patient) basis.\@ [498]
Subgroup analyses based on 30-day readmissions and lengths of stay greater than 5 days were also performed using the same metrics.\@ [519]
[519]
Model performances were assessed with metrics appropriate to the prediction endpoint.\@ [530]
For binary outcomes %including readmission and length of stay, death within 48-72 hours of hospitalization, gender, race, and financial class, [533]
the Brier score loss, area under the receiver operator characteristic curve (ROC AUC), and average precision were calculated.\@ [551]
Additionally, ROC curves, precision-recall curves, calibration plots, and confusion matrices were generated for visual assessments of model performance.\@ [569]
Lower Brier scores are better, with a score <0.25 generally considered indicative of a useful model.\supercite{Steyerberg2010}\@ [586]
They are calculated as the mean squared difference between the probabilty assigned to each sample and the actual outcome (1 or 0).\@ [608]
Calibration curves provide a visual representation of a similar concept, and show the model's predicted probability vs.\ [625]
the fraction of samples at that probability with the actual outcome.\@ [636]
The curve of perfectly calibrated model would exhibit a straight 45\degree\ line.\@ [648]
ROC curves, the corresponding ROC AUC, and precision-recall curves, with the average precision metric, show classification performance [665]
at all possible classification thresholds.\supercite{pencina2015evaluating} Higher numbers are better.\@ [675]
ROC AUC and average precision of 0.5 indicate a model that performs no better than chance.\@ [691]
An AUC of 1.0 indicates 100\% true positive and 0\% false positive rates, [704]
while an average precision of 1.0 indicates a positive predictive value of 100\%.\@ [717]
Confusion matrices show the number of samples correctly and incorrectly classified.\@ [728]

[728]
For numeric outcomes including length of stay in days, days until readmission, and age, we calculated [744]
root mean square error (RMSE), median absolute error, mean absolute error, and R\textsuperscript{2} scores.\@ [759]
RMSE is calculated as the square root of the average of squared errors, or difference between observed and expected values, [779]
and yields a metric in the same units as the predictive target (here, days or years).\supercite{chai2014root}\@ [796]
Median absolute error is the median absolute difference between the predicted target and the actual value, [812]
and mean absolute error is the mean of the same.\@ [822]
R\textsuperscript{2} scores are the percentage of the target variable variation captured by the model, where 100\% indicates [840]
